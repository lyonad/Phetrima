What is Phetrima?

Phetrima is a comprehensive forecasting research platform that evaluates two powerful time series forecasting models: Prophet and ARIMA. This project analyzes economic growth patterns across 117 countries to determine which model provides better GDP predictions.

The Goal

Compare the predictive accuracy of Prophet (Facebook's forecasting tool) and ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) models for forecasting GDP across different countries and continents.

The Data

We use annual GDP data from the World Bank spanning 25 years (2000-2025) for 117 countries. The training period is 2000-2021, and we test predictions on 2022-2025.

The Method

Each model generates GDP forecasts for each country, and we measure accuracy using three metrics: MAE, RMSE, and MAPE. The model with lower errors wins for that country.

The Results

Prophet performs better in 72 countries (61.5%), while ARIMA excels in 45 countries (38.5%). Prophet particularly shines in developing economies with non-linear growth patterns.

Understanding the Technical Terms

ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average)

What it means: ARIMA is a classical statistical model used for time series forecasting.

  • AutoRegressive (AR): Uses past values to predict future values
  • Integrated (I): Handles trends in data by taking differences
  • Moving Average (MA): Smooths out random fluctuations

In simple terms: ARIMA finds patterns in past GDP data to predict future GDP. It works best when data has clear, consistent patterns over time.

Prophet

What it means: Prophet is Facebook's open-source forecasting tool designed to handle time series data with seasonal patterns and trends.

  • Trend Component: Captures long-term direction of GDP (growing, declining)
  • Seasonality: Identifies recurring patterns (though not relevant for annual GDP)
  • Changepoints: Automatically detects major shifts in trends

In simple terms: Prophet is like a smart trend detector. It excels at catching sudden changes in economic growth, like the post-COVID recovery. It's particularly good for developing countries with rapid economic changes.

MAE (Mean Absolute Error)

What it measures: The average difference between predicted GDP and actual GDP.

Example: If Prophet predicts a country's GDP at $100 billion but it's actually $110 billion, the error is $10 billion. We calculate this for all forecast years and take the average.

Lower is better: A smaller MAE means the model's predictions are closer to reality.

RMSE (Root Mean Square Error)

What it measures: Similar to MAE but penalizes larger errors more heavily.

Why it matters: If one model has a few very bad predictions, RMSE will highlight this more than MAE would.

Lower is better: Indicates the model avoids large prediction mistakes.

MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error)

What it measures: The error as a percentage of the actual value.

Example: If actual GDP is $100 billion and we predicted $110 billion, the MAPE is 10%.

Why it's useful: Allows us to compare accuracy across countries of different sizes. A $10 billion error for the USA (tiny GDP) is less significant than for a small country.

Lower is better: Lower percentage means more accurate predictions relative to the country's GDP size.

GDP (Gross Domestic Product)

What it is: The total value of all goods and services produced in a country in a year.

Why it matters: GDP is the most important indicator of economic health. Predicting future GDP helps governments plan budgets, businesses make investment decisions, and economists understand growth trends.

In this study: We're trying to predict each country's annual GDP for years 2022-2025 using data from 2000-2021.

SARIMAX

What it means: Seasonal ARIMA with eXogenous variables - an advanced version of ARIMA used in our study.

In practice: We use SARIMAX for ARIMA modeling, though we focus on non-seasonal patterns since GDP is annual data.

Grid Search: The model tests many different parameter combinations (called "orders") to find the best fit for each country's data.

Changepoints

What they are: Points in time where the trend changes direction or speed significantly.

Example: COVID-19 was a major changepoint - GDP dropped dramatically in 2020 for most countries. Prophet automatically detects these.

Why it matters: For countries with rapid economic changes (like China or India), changepoints are crucial for accurate forecasting.

How Phetrima Works

2

Determine which model performed better for each country and generate insights

Key Findings at a Glance

Prophet Dominates Globally

Prophet wins in 72 out of 117 countries (61.5%), showing superior performance in most economies worldwide.

Lower Errors Across Metrics

Prophet achieves lower MAE (76.0B vs 101.4B), lower RMSE (80.6B vs 106.8B), and lower MAPE (11.4% vs 13.6%) compared to ARIMA.

Best for Developing Economies

Prophet excels in Asia, Africa, and rapidly growing economies with non-linear growth patterns (China, India, Indonesia).

ARIMA Holds Its Own

ARIMA performs better in stable, mature economies like the USA, Japan, and France where patterns are consistent.

Regional Patterns Matter

Asia shows massive Prophet advantage (77% improvement), while North America is more competitive due to USA's scale.

Context Is Key

The best model depends on country characteristics: volatility, trend patterns, data quality, and economic stability.

Insights & Key Findings

Conclusions and recommendations from the research

Key Findings

  • Prophet excels globally with MAE ~76.0 billion USD vs ARIMA ~101.4 billion USD
  • Prophet wins in 72 countries (61.5%), ARIMA in 45 countries (38.5%)
  • Prophet has lower MAPE (11.4%) compared to ARIMA (13.6%)

Prophet Excels In

  • Non-linear growth economies like China, India, Indonesia
  • Countries with post-pandemic trends with sharp recovery
  • Rapidly developing economies with significant changepoints
  • Asia: Prophet 108.2B vs ARIMA 192.1B (44% improvement)

ARIMA Better For

  • Stable economies like United States, Brazil, Japan
  • Data with high volatility but strong short-term patterns
  • Small datasets with low noise
  • North America: Competitive performance on large economies

By Continent (Average RMSE)

  • Africa: ARIMA 26.3B vs Prophet 22.5B
  • Asia: ARIMA 200B vs Prophet 112B
  • Europe: ARIMA 55.3B vs Prophet 39.9B
  • North America: Competitive, scale dependent

Recommendations

1

Production Strategy

Use Prophet as the baseline for global forecasting, with hierarchical models per region to adjust for scale

2

Using ARIMA

Maintain ARIMA for countries with stable data. Consider integrating exogenous variables (VAR/ARIMAX) for volatile countries

3

Further Evaluation

Implement rolling origin validation and explore boosting (XGBoost, LSTM) for multi-year horizons

4

Data Normalization

Transform GDP to log or growth rate to stabilize variance before advanced modeling

Methodology

Dataset

Annual GDP data 2000-2025 from World Bank for 117 countries (after filtering < 15 observations)

Data Split

Training: 2000-2021 | Testing: 2022-2025 (4 years horizon)

ARIMA Model

Grid search SARIMAX with orders (p,d,q) from 0-2, without seasonal component. Selection based on AIC

Prophet Model

Prophet with multiplicative seasonality mode, yearly frequency, without weekly/daily seasonality

Evaluation Metrics

MAE (Mean Absolute Error), RMSE (Root Mean Square Error), MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error)

Research Limitations

  • Short test horizon (2022-2025) with high sensitivity to COVID-19 shock
  • No inflation or PPP adjustment - nominal GDP values may be biased across countries
  • Prophet requires high runtime for 100+ countries - parallel optimization recommended
  • Does not include external variables (commodity prices, trade, policy) that affect GDP
  • Baseline models without extensive hyperparameter tuning

About This Project

What inspired me to create this project was my curiosity about how current AI forecasting models could predict economic conditions based on historical data. In my exploration, I discovered two powerful time series forecasting algorithms: ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) and Prophet (developed by Meta).

I wanted to compare these models in real-world cases, which led to the creation of this project—"Phetrima" (Prophet + ARIMA). Through comprehensive analysis and comparison across 117 countries with 25 years of GDP data, I found that the Prophet model made by Meta is indeed better than ARIMA for time forecasting based on historical data.

This project demonstrates the practical application of data science and machine learning in economic forecasting, showcasing how different algorithms perform when faced with real-world complexity and uncertainty.
